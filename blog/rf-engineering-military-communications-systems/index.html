RF Engineering in Military Communications: From Tactical Radios to Electronic Warfare

Published 2026-02-25

The electromagnetic spectrum has become the invisible fifth domain of warfare — as critical to modern military operations as land, sea, air, and cyber. Every command transmitted between units, every radar pulse scanning the horizon, every satellite link carrying intelligence data, and every electronic warfare system jamming enemy communications depends on radio frequency engineering. The defence sector's investment in RF capabilities has accelerated dramatically, driven by peer-competitor threats and the realization that electromagnetic superiority may be the single most decisive factor in future conflicts.

Understanding how RF engineering shapes modern military communications and electronic warfare capabilities provides critical context for defence planners, procurement officials, and strategic analysts assessing force readiness and technological advantage.

The Evolution of Military RF Communications

Military radio communications have evolved through distinct generations, each driven by advances in RF engineering. The journey from World War II's bulky vacuum tube radios operating on fixed frequencies to today's cognitive software-defined systems that autonomously hop across thousands of frequencies per second represents one of the most consequential technology progressions in military history.

The current generation of tactical radios — exemplified by programs like the US Army's HMS Manpack and the UK's Bowman successor program, Morpheus — are fundamentally different from their predecessors. These systems implement wideband waveforms that span hundreds of megahertz, mesh networking protocols that automatically route traffic around destroyed nodes, and adaptive modulation schemes that adjust data rates based on real-time channel conditions. The RF engineering required to achieve reliable communications across contested electromagnetic environments while maintaining interoperability between coalition partners represents some of the most demanding work in the field.

DARPA's Communications in Contested Environments (C2E) program has invested heavily in developing RF technologies that maintain communications even under active electronic attack. The technical approaches include spread-spectrum techniques that distribute signals below the noise floor, making them virtually undetectable; directional antennas that concentrate energy toward intended recipients while minimizing exposure to adversary intercept; and cognitive radio algorithms that continuously sense the electromagnetic environment and adapt transmission parameters to avoid jammed frequencies.

Electronic Warfare: The RF Battlespace

Electronic warfare — the military discipline of controlling the electromagnetic spectrum — has moved from a supporting capability to a decisive one. NATO's Joint Electronic Warfare doctrine recognizes three pillars: electronic attack (EA), electronic protection (EP), and electronic warfare support (ES). Each pillar demands specialized RF engineering expertise spanning antenna design, signal processing, propagation analysis, and system integration.

Electronic attack encompasses jamming — transmitting RF energy to disrupt enemy communications, radar, or navigation systems — and deception, which involves generating false signals to mislead adversary sensors. Modern jammers are far more sophisticated than the brute-force noise generators of previous decades. Techniques like digital RF memory (DRFM) jamming capture an incoming radar pulse, modify it, and retransmit it with precise timing to create convincing false targets. Reactive jamming systems detect enemy transmissions, classify them in microseconds, and generate optimized jamming waveforms tailored to the specific threat — a capability that requires extraordinary RF signal processing performance.

The electronic protection side focuses on hardening friendly systems against enemy electronic attack. Techniques include frequency hopping at rates exceeding thousands of hops per second, spread-spectrum waveforms that resist narrowband jamming, adaptive nulling antennas that place reception nulls in the direction of jammers, and error-correction coding that maintains communications even when portions of the signal are corrupted. Each technique requires deep RF engineering knowledge to implement effectively without degrading the primary communications capability.

Electronic warfare support — the intelligence-gathering pillar — involves detecting, intercepting, locating, and analyzing adversary RF emissions. Modern signals intelligence (SIGINT) systems must monitor enormous swaths of spectrum simultaneously, detecting signals of interest buried in dense electromagnetic environments. Wideband digital receivers using high-speed analog-to-digital converters can now capture and digitize gigahertz of bandwidth for real-time analysis, enabling identification and geolocation of emitters that would have been invisible to previous-generation systems.

Radar Systems: The RF Foundation of Situational Awareness

Radar remains the primary sensor for military situational awareness, and modern radar systems represent the pinnacle of RF engineering achievement. The transition from mechanically steered dish antennas to active electronically scanned arrays (AESA) has been transformative. An AESA radar contains hundreds to thousands of individual transmit/receive modules, each with its own miniature RF chain including amplifier, phase shifter, and circulator. By controlling the phase and amplitude of each element independently, the radar can steer its beam electronically in microseconds — compared to the seconds required for mechanical steering — enabling simultaneous tracking of hundreds of targets across multiple sectors.

The AN/APG-81 radar in the F-35 Lightning II represents the current state of the art, with over 1,200 transmit/receive modules operating across multiple frequency bands. Beyond traditional air-to-air and air-to-ground radar functions, the system performs electronic warfare, communications, and identification friend-or-foe operations simultaneously — a convergence of capabilities made possible by the flexibility of its underlying RF architecture. Raytheon, Northrop Grumman, and BAE Systems have invested billions in gallium nitride (GaN) semiconductor technology that enables these modules to produce more power, operate across wider bandwidths, and dissipate heat more efficiently than previous gallium arsenide designs.

Ground-based air defence radars face their own unique RF engineering challenges. Systems like the Patriot's AN/MPQ-65 and the Russian S-400's suite of radars must detect targets with minimal radar cross-sections — including stealth aircraft designed to deflect and absorb radar energy — at ranges sufficient to enable effective engagement. The RF engineering solutions include ultra-low sidelobes to reject ground clutter and jamming, advanced waveform design that optimizes detection probability for specific target characteristics, and multi-static architectures that use spatially separated transmitters and receivers to defeat stealth shaping.

Satellite Communications and Beyond-Line-of-Sight Connectivity

Military satellite communications (MILSATCOM) provide the beyond-line-of-sight connectivity essential for global force coordination. The RF engineering challenges are immense: ground terminals must communicate with satellites hundreds or thousands of kilometers away through an atmosphere that absorbs, scatters, and refracts radio waves. Rain attenuation alone can cause signal losses exceeding 10 dB at Ka-band frequencies used by high-throughput military satellites, requiring adaptive coding and modulation, site diversity, and significant link margin in the system design.

The US Department of Defense's Protected Tactical Enterprise Service (PTES) and the UK's Skynet 6 program represent the next generation of protected MILSATCOM, designed to maintain connectivity even under electronic attack from sophisticated adversaries. These systems use anti-jam techniques including antenna nulling, frequency hopping across wide bandwidths, and spread-spectrum processing gains that make the communications link resistant to all but the most powerful dedicated jammers.

The emergence of commercial low-Earth orbit constellations — particularly SpaceX's Starlink, which has demonstrated military utility in Ukraine — is disrupting traditional MILSATCOM architectures. The RF system design challenges of integrating commercial LEO capacity with dedicated military satellites require new approaches to spectrum management, terminal design, and network architecture that the defence acquisition community is actively working to address.

Spectrum Operations and Electromagnetic Battle Management

Modern militaries increasingly recognize that managing the electromagnetic spectrum is an operational challenge comparable to managing airspace or cyberspace. The US Army's electromagnetic spectrum operations (EMSO) doctrine, updated in 2025, elevates spectrum management from an administrative function to a warfighting discipline with its own planning processes, dedicated staff elements, and real-time battle management tools.

Electromagnetic battle management systems integrate data from SIGINT sensors, radar warning receivers, communications monitoring, and electronic warfare systems to build a real-time picture of the electromagnetic environment — what the US military calls the electromagnetic operational environment (EMOE). This picture enables commanders to deconflict friendly emissions, identify adversary electronic order of battle, plan electronic attack operations, and assess the effectiveness of electronic warfare actions.

The RF engineering challenges of electromagnetic battle management are substantial. The systems must process and fuse data from sensors operating across the spectrum from HF to millimeter wave, correlate emissions with specific platforms and units, and present actionable information to operators in near-real-time. The computational requirements alone — processing billions of signal samples per second from distributed sensors — push the boundaries of current digital signal processing technology.

Emerging RF Technologies in Defence

Several emerging RF technologies promise to reshape military capabilities over the coming decade. Cognitive electronic warfare systems that use machine learning to autonomously classify threats and generate optimal countermeasures are transitioning from research programs to operational deployment. DARPA's Adaptive RF Counter-measures (ARC) program has demonstrated systems that can learn and counter previously unseen radar waveforms in real-time, without pre-programmed threat libraries.

Photonic RF systems that use optical technology for signal generation, distribution, and processing offer dramatic improvements in bandwidth, dynamic range, and immunity to electromagnetic interference. These systems can transmit wideband RF signals over fiber optic links with minimal distortion, enabling distributed antenna architectures where processing is centralized while antenna elements are spread across a platform or installation.

Quantum sensing technologies, while still in early development, promise RF receivers with sensitivity approaching fundamental physical limits. DARPA and the UK's Defence Science and Technology Laboratory (DSTL) are investing in Rydberg atom-based electric field sensors that can detect RF signals across enormous bandwidths with unprecedented sensitivity — potentially enabling detection of low-probability-of-intercept communications and stealth platform emissions that are invisible to current receiver technology.

The Strategic Imperative

The nations and alliances that maintain superiority in RF engineering capabilities will hold a decisive advantage in future conflicts. The electromagnetic spectrum is the medium through which modern forces communicate, sense their environment, and deny those capabilities to adversaries. Investment in RF engineering talent, research infrastructure, and operational doctrine is not optional — it is a strategic imperative on par with investments in cyber capabilities, space systems, and autonomous platforms.

As the electromagnetic battlespace grows more complex and contested, the premium on sophisticated RF engineering will only increase. The defence organizations that build deep RF expertise — both in their uniformed technical corps and in their industrial base of specialized engineering firms — will be best positioned to operate effectively in the invisible domain that increasingly determines the outcome of visible ones.
